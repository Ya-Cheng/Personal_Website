Boston University High School Honors Program

Jul. - Aug. 2022 Summer

This summer, I participated in Boston University’s High School Honors Program. In fact, the HSH program is the longest program among all BU’s summer programs, and the dorm was always a huge combination of students from different programs including internship, dancing, and science-research programs. I had seen so many interesting things, met a variety of people, and gained numerous cool experiences that would never be erased from my memories.

Boston is a fascinating city. The weather is never rainy, the daytime is much longer than the daytime in Taiwan, the air is dry and cool, and the atmosphere is so leisurely that I even doubted Boston as a “city”. Most of the buildings and residences are much older than normal cities, but they somehow conserved their own culture and beauty. Generally, Boston is separated into downtown Boston in the southeast region and the school regions; Boston University is located in Western Boston, whereas Harvard and MIT are located in the north of the Charles River. For preparation, the first thing when my father and I arrived in Boston was to get familiar with Boston’s Train system (people in Boston called it “T”), which was a clever decision because the Train was also the main transportation in BU and it would be very convenient to get to anywhere after I moved into BU’s dorm. Besides all of that, one thing that made me suffer for the first two weeks was the Americans’ value of distance. The average distance among buildings where I had classes and meals was roughly 20 to 25 minutes walk, which was especially difficult for me who is living in a country where everything is about a 5 minute walk. I was even tricked by my roommates when I was searching for Target in the university for the first time; they told me that it was a ten minute walk, but actually it took me almost 30 minutes to get there! Thus, I realized how important the Train was within the large BU campus (and it is free for BU students to take the Train).

For the summer program, I took two computer science courses: Introduction to Programming, which is mainly about coding with Python, and the Introduction to Web Programming. Both of the courses were substantial and fulfilling, and the professors and TAs were extremely friendly and helpful to us. During office hours, I often discuss with my classmates and the professor about my assignments and class materials. This not only increased my efficiency on doing assignments, but also gave me chances to listen to stories from those college students (who really studied CS at BU for semesters). They all have unique backgrounds, personalities and different stories and reasons for coming to BU. However, they were all surprised to know my age, since according to most of them, they didn’t even begin to learn coding until their senior year at high school, and one BU junior didn’t even have his own computer when he was at my age. All in all, they encouraged me to keep my initial goal and motivation because computer science is never a joyful path. It is their positiveness that helps them to go through those harsh processes and competitions.

Boston University is a friendly and diverse community. On my floor of the dorm, there were classmates from other US states, Europe, India, China, and even Korea, and I was actually the only student from Taiwan within the entire HSH summer program. Nevertheless, this diversity did not hinder our interactions. Although we all have different cultures, religions, backgrounds, and values, all of us were willing to share our past experiences and the things we learned from our own courses or research. By the end of the six weeks, I became best friends with a few Asian students from Shenzhen and Los Angeles, and a girl from Portugal. We are still keeping in touch and sharing fun things in our daily lives.
